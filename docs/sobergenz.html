Does Gen Z drink less than other generations did?

Author

Lily Dunk

Published

March 17, 2026

Background

In the past few years, I’ve read various news articles about a supposed trend: that Gen Z (people born between 1997 and 2012) drink less alcohol than previous generations did at the same age:

  • The Conversation, “The rise of ‘sober curiosity:’ Why Gen Zers are reducing their alcohol consumption” (November 2024)
  • Time Magazine, “Why Gen Z Is Drinking Less” (January 2025)
  • Gallup, “U.S. Drinking Rate at New Low as Alcohol Concerns Surge” (August 2025)
  • Wall Street Journal, “Why Generation Z Doesn’t Drink Much” (September 2025)
  • USA Today, “Gen Z is ghosting alcohol and changing what a ‘night out’ looks like” (March 2026)

These articles reference data from various surveys, and I was curious if I could identify that same trend in the CDC’s annual Behavioral Health Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) survey.

The main questions these articles raise for me are:

Compared to previous generations at the same age…

  • Is Gen Z more likely to be completely sober? (meaning, to not drink at all)?
  • Does Gen Z drink less often?
  • Is Gen Z less likely to binge drink?

and, if so, is there a greater difference in Gen Z than in other generations? In other words, can the difference be explained by all Americans reducing their drinking, or is this a Gen Z-specific phenomenon?

About the data

Data from the annual BRFSS surveys are publicly available from the CDC. The most recent dataset is from the 2024 survey. Due to changes in survey methodology, the CDC advises that recent BRFSS data cannot be directly compared with BRFSS data older than the 2011 survey. So we will compare the 2024 and 2011 surveys.

The Pew Research Center uses a standard definition of each generation by birth year:

Generation Birth years Age in 2024
Generation Z 1997 onward 21 to 27
Millennials 1981 to 1996 27 to 43
Generation X 1965 to 1980 43 to 59
Baby Boomers 1946 to 1964 59 to 78
Silent Generation 1928 to 1945 78 to 96

Gen Z was born from 1997 onward, so in 2024, the oldest members of Gen Z would have been age 27. Millennials born in 1996 could also have been age 27 in 2024 depending on their birthdate, but for the sake of simplicity, I will group 27-year-old participants with Gen Z.

I also want to exclude people under the legal drinking age, because I suspect that underage drinking could change because of changes to law enforcement, university discipline policies, etc. So our Gen Z sample will be ages 21 to 27, and everyone else will be ages 27+.

This means that our two samples of people ages 21 to 27 will include:

  • those born between 1997 and 2003 (Gen Z), surveyed in 2024, compared to
  • those born between 1984 and 1990 (Millennials), surveyed in 2011

The BRFSS variables we can use are:

_IMPAGE _AGE80 2011: Age value used to determine age groups; 2024: Imputed Age value collapsed above 80 The 2011 dataset does not collapse high ages, but this shouldn’t affect our analysis because we are only interested in young people. Both samples are imputed, meaning that if a participant did not report their age, the CDC substituted a statistical estimate of their age.

Next, we’ll recode the age and alcohol variables based on the CDC’s codebooks for each sample. For the proportion of days that participants drink, we’ll convert to the number of days that participants drink per month (30 days) for clarity.

brfss <- brfss3 |>
  dplyr::filter(age >= 21) |>
  dplyr::mutate(
    age_group = dplyr::case_when(
      age <= 27 ~ "Age 21-27",
      age >= 28 ~ "Age 28+"
    ) |>
      forcats::fct(levels = c("Age 21-27", "Age 28+")),
    #' Per the CDC's data documentation, 900 indicates "Don't know/Not sure" or
    #' "Refused/Missing", so we will recode it to NA
    drink_days = dplyr::if_else(
      drink_days == 900,
      NA,
      drink_days
    ) * (30/100), # divide by 100 to get proportion, multiply by 30 days
    drink = drink_days > 0,
    binge_drink = dplyr::recode_values(
      binge_drink,
      1 ~ FALSE,
      2 ~ TRUE,
      9 ~ NA
    )
  )

Regression model walkthrough

For each variable, we’re not just testing if drinking increased from 2011 to 2024; we’re testing if drinking increased from 2011 to 2024 more for the 21-27 age group than it did for the 28+ age group. Since we are looking at the relationship between three different variables (alcohol use, age group, and sample year), we’ll use a regression analysis instead of a more typical statistical test like a chi-squared test or a t-test.

We’ll create a generalized linear model using the survey package to predict each variable based on age group, sample year, and an interaction between the two. We can then use the regression coefficients to understand how those predictors affect the outcome.

brfss_glm1 <- survey::svyglm(
  formula = drink ~ age_group * sample_year,
  design = brfss_svy

The intercept is the estimate for the baseline groups: age 21-27 and sample year 2011.

The age_group coefficient is the estimated difference between the age groups when the sample year is kept constant (2011). The sample_year coefficient is the estimated difference between the sample years when the age group is kept constant (Age 21-27). The p-values for both of these coefficients are <0.001, so we know that there is a significant difference between the age groups in 2011 and a significant difference between the sample years for Age 21-27.

The interaction coefficient tells us about both variables. This is the difference between the differences: Between 2011 and 2024, the proportion of participants who drink decreased by 7% less for Age 28+ than it did for Age 21-27. And because the p-value for the interaction coefficient is also <0.001, we know that the difference is statistically significant.
